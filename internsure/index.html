UNDER THE HOOD

What the engine looks for

Six signals, drawn from how real internship scams are actually run.

Website legitimacy

Flags offers with no verifiable company website, or one that doesn't match the brand being claimed.

Contact domain

Checks whether you're being contacted from a free address (Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook) instead of an official company domain.

Payment requests

Treats any upfront "registration", "kit" or "processing" fee as a major warning sign — legitimate internships don't charge.

Stipend realism

Compares the promised stipend against realistic ranges to catch "too good to be true" bait offers.

Description completeness

Vague, copy-pasted, or suspiciously thin job descriptions push the risk score up.

Privacy by design

There's no server. Every check runs as JavaScript in your browser tab, and nothing is logged or transmitted.

KNOW THE PATTERN

Common Internship Scams

Most fraudulent offers fall into one of these familiar shapes.

The upfront fee trap

"Registration", "training kit" or "background verification" charges, collected before any work starts.

The too-good stipend

An unusually high payout for unskilled, low-effort work — designed purely to get you to commit fast.

Chat-only recruiting

The entire process happens over WhatsApp or Telegram, with no official email or company portal involved.

Pay-for-certificate

You "complete" the internship, then are told the certificate is released only after a final payment.

Vague "earn from home"

Generic data-entry or "easy money" listings with no named company, no interview, and no real product.

Instant offer, zero screening

An offer letter arrives with no interview at all — often just a way to collect personal documents.

BEFORE YOU APPLY

Safety Tips for Students

Small habits that catch most scams before any money changes hands.

  • Search the company on LinkedIn and Google before replying — a real employer has a public footprint.
  • Never pay any fee to "secure" an internship, a stipend, or a certificate.
  • Insist on a written offer letter on official letterhead, not just a chat message.
  • Confirm the recruiter's identity and check that emails come from the company's own domain.
  • Ask to speak with a current or former intern before accepting.
  • Never share OTPs, passwords, or full Aadhaar/PAN numbers over chat or email.
  • Cross-check the stipend against typical rates for that role and city.
  • If the process feels rushed or too easy, slow down — legitimate hiring rarely is.
